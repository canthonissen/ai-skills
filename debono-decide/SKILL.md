# De Bono — Decide (focus & choose)

Two stages of the spine live here:
- **TO** — fix the **aim** before anything else (AGO).
- **SO** — **narrow** the field to a choice (FIP, values, the decision procedure).

The governing principle: **do the thinking first, let emotion choose last.** A decision
is ultimately made by values and feeling — that's not a flaw, it's the point. But the
feeling should arrive *after* you've explored, so it's choosing between well-understood
options rather than reacting to the first one.

---

## AGO — Aims, Goals, Objectives

**What it is.** Make the purpose explicit: *"What am I actually trying to achieve here?"*

**Why it matters.** A surprising amount of action runs on **"because" reasons** — habit
("we always have"), imitation ("everyone does"), or reaction ("they did X, so we do Y").
Those aren't aims. Until the aim is named, you can't tell whether any option is good,
because "good" only means "serves the aim."

**How to do it.**
1. Ask "what am I trying to achieve?" — write the aim in one plain sentence.
2. Separate it into levels if useful: the broad **aim**, the specific **goals**, the
   immediate **objectives**.
3. Check for hidden or competing aims (the stated aim vs. the real one — e.g. "inform the
   team" vs. "cover myself").
4. Test every option later against this aim, not against habit.

**Example (work).** "We need a new logo." AGO: what's the aim? If it's "look modern,"
that's a design brief. If it's really "win back lapsed customers," a logo may be the
wrong move entirely. Naming the aim changes the whole project.

**Add the mirror: the anti-goal.** Pair AGO with its inverse (`debono-invert`) — name where
you must *not* end up. The aim says where to go; the anti-goal fences off the outcomes you
refuse to allow, which often constrains the choice more sharply than the aim itself.

---

## FIP — First Important Priorities

**What it is.** After you've generated factors, options, or consequences, deliberately
pick the **few that matter most.** "Of all these, which are the First Important
Priorities, and why?"

**Why it matters.** Not everything weighs the same. Treating every factor as equal is its
own kind of failure — it scatters attention and stalls decisions. FIP is the squeeze from
"everything matters" to "these 2–3 decide it."

**How to do it.**
1. Take your list (from CAF, PMI, APC, C&S — wherever it came from).
2. Choose the top **2–4** items that matter most.
3. State *why* each is a priority — the reason is the useful part.
4. If two options score the same on the priorities, the priorities aren't sharp enough
   yet; refine them.

**Example (personal).** Job offer with ten factors. FIP narrows to three that actually
decide it: long-term learning, commute, and whether you respect the manager. Salary,
which felt central, drops to "good enough threshold, not a deciding factor." Now the
choice is clear.

## Emotions & Values check

**What it is.** A deliberate pass to surface the **feelings and values** in play, *named
out loud*, before the final choice.

**Why it matters.** Values and emotions are the real currency of a decision — pretending
they're not there just means they operate in the dark. De Bono distinguishes:
- **Background emotions/mood** — the state you're already in (tired, defensive, excited).
  Ask: is my mood doing the choosing here?
- **Emotions brought on by *perception*** — how you're *seeing* the situation triggers the
  feeling. Change the perception (with explore/create tools) and the feeling can change.
  So if a strong emotion is driving you, first check you've actually looked (PMI/CAF).
- **Values** — what you care about: security, fairness, growth, freedom, loyalty, status.

**How to do it.**
1. Name the emotions present, and which are *background mood* vs. *response to how you see
   it.*
2. List the **values at stake**, and which are high-value vs. low-value here.
3. Ask: "If I weren't in this mood, would the options look different?" and "Which option
   best serves my high values?"

**Example (personal/ethical).** Tempting offer that means leaving a loyal teammate in the
lurch. Emotion: excitement (foreground) + guilt. Values: ambition vs. loyalty. Naming them
turns "I just feel weird about it" into a real question you can actually decide.

## The decision procedure

When it's a genuine decision, run this sequence. Each step names the tool/skill to use.

1. **Aim** — AGO. What are we deciding, and to what end?
2. **Options** — do you have enough? If only one, go to `debono-create` (APC) first.
3. **Field** — CAF (`debono-explore`): what factors bear on this?
4. **Test the front-runners** — PMI on each serious option (`debono-explore`).
5. **Time** — C&S (`debono-explore`): how do the options age?
6. **People** — OPV (`debono-explore`): who's affected, how do they see it?
7. **Priorities** — FIP: which 2–3 factors actually decide it?
8. **Values** — the Emotions & Values check above.
9. **Choose** — pick the option that best serves the *aim* and *high-priority values*.
   Then let the feeling confirm it. If you can't feel settled, you usually skipped a step.
10. **Invert** — run a quick **pre-mortem** (`debono-invert`): assume the option you're
    leaning toward has failed badly — why? If a failure mode is irreversible, fix it or
    change course before you commit.
11. **Act** — hand to `debono-act` to turn the choice into action.

You won't always need every step — for a small decision, AGO + PMI + FIP is plenty. Scale
the machinery to the stakes.

**Example (work, compressed).** Whether to hire contractor A or B.
AGO: "ship the launch on time without a quality drop." PMI favors A on speed, B on
quality. C&S: B's quality compounds over the year; A's speed only helps this quarter.
FIP: the two priorities are launch date and code we can maintain. Values: we value
durability over heroics. Choose B, and de-risk the date by cutting launch scope (an action
for `debono-act`).
