# De Bono — Explore (broaden perception)

These are **attention-directing tools**. Each one deliberately points your attention at
an area you would otherwise skip — like forcing yourself to look North, then South, then
East, then West before saying what's around you. Run them **before** you judge.

Rules for all four:
- **Brief and deliberate** — about 2–3 minutes each. Set a timer if it helps.
- **Scan, don't argue.** You're collecting points, not building a case. Write a quick
  list; don't polish.
- **Genuinely look.** The discipline is to find points you do *not* already believe.
- **One tool at a time.** Finish the scan before reacting to it.

Pick the tool by what's happening:

| What's happening | Tool |
|------------------|------|
| A specific idea/proposal is on the table | **PMI** |
| About to plan, judge, or choose; want the full field | **CAF** |
| The choice plays out over time | **C&S** |
| Other people are involved or affected | **OPV** |

You can run several in sequence (CAF to map the field, then PMI on the leading option,
then OPV for the people, then C&S on consequences).

---

## PMI — Plus, Minus, Interesting

**What it is.** Scan an idea in three deliberate sweeps: the **Plus** points (good),
then the **Minus** points (bad), then the **Interesting** points (neither good nor bad —
"it would be interesting to see what happens if…", consequences, where it leads, what it
changes).

**Why it matters.** Without it, your emotional reaction decides everything in the first
second and then your thinking just defends that reaction. PMI delays the verdict so the
verdict is informed. De Bono's famous demonstration: he asked 30 schoolchildren about
"every pupil gets paid $5 a week to attend school." All 30 loved it. After doing a PMI,
29 of 30 had reversed — they thought of the Minus and Interesting points themselves
(where would the money come from, would pocket money stop, would older kids take it).

**How to do it.**
1. State the idea in one line.
2. **P:** list every plus point. (2 min)
3. **M:** list every minus point. (2 min)
4. **I:** list what's merely *interesting* — questions, side-effects, "what if this led
   to…". (1–2 min)
5. *Only now* react. What's your view, given the whole scan?

**Don't** use PMI to pad a verdict you've already reached. If you only find Plus points,
you haven't done the Minus sweep honestly.

**Go further with inversion.** A Minus scan asks "what's bad about this?" To catch the
risks it misses, follow with a *failure inversion* (`debono-invert`): "if I wanted to
guarantee this fails, how would I do it — and am I doing any of that?"

**Example (work).** Idea: "Switch the team to a four-day week."
- P: more rest, recruiting edge, focus, fewer sick days.
- M: client cover on Fridays, same workload compressed, payroll questions.
- I: interesting to see if output actually drops; might force us to kill low-value
  meetings; could change how we measure productivity.
Verdict shifts from "love it" to "worth a 3-month trial with clear output metrics."

## CAF — Consider All Factors

**What it is.** Ask: *"What are all the factors I should take into account here — what
am I leaving out?"* PMI evaluates one idea; CAF maps the whole field of factors before
you evaluate anything.

**Why it matters.** Most bad decisions come from a factor that was simply never
considered, not from weighing known factors wrongly. CAF is the cheap insurance against
the obvious-in-hindsight miss.

**How to do it.**
1. Name the decision or situation.
2. List every factor that bears on it — yours, other people's, practical, financial,
   emotional, long-term.
3. Then ask the key second question: *"What has been left out? What would someone else
   say I've missed?"*
4. Mark any factor you'd be in trouble for ignoring.

**Example (personal).** Choosing a flat to rent. Obvious factors: rent, size, location.
CAF surfaces the missed ones: commute *at rush hour*, noise, damp, notice period,
parking, whether the landlord is responsive, total move-in cost, can you actually get out
of the lease early. The missed factor is usually the one that bites.

## C&S — Consequence & Sequel

**What it is.** Look **forward in time** at the consequences of an action or decision,
across four horizons:
- **Immediate** (right now)
- **Short-term** (roughly 1–5 years)
- **Medium-term** (roughly 5–25 years)
- **Long-term** (25 years+)

**Why it matters.** We're wired to weigh immediate effects and discount later ones. Many
choices look great immediately and poor later (or vice-versa). C&S deliberately drags
attention down the timeline.

**How to do it.**
1. State the action.
2. List consequences at each of the four horizons in turn.
3. Note especially where the sign flips — good now, bad later, or the reverse.

**Example (learning / career).** Action: "Take the higher-paying job that involves no
new learning."
- Immediate: more money, relief.
- Short-term: comfortable, savings grow.
- Medium-term: skills stagnate while the field moves on.
- Long-term: harder to move, narrower options.
The immediate Plus and the long-term Minus point in opposite directions — that's the
real decision.

## OPV — Other People's Views

**What it is.** Thinking is rarely just about you. List the people involved in or
affected by the situation, then **step into each one's shoes** and state how *they* see
it, what *their* aims are, what *they* would call a good outcome.

**Why it matters.** Your view is one perception among several. Seeing the others' real
view (not a strawman) is the difference between persuading/negotiating well and talking
past people. It also surfaces factors you can't see from your own seat.

**How to do it.**
1. List everyone with a stake (don't forget the quiet or absent ones).
2. For each, write 1–2 lines *as them*: their aim, their worry, what "winning" means to
   them.
3. Note where views genuinely conflict vs. where they only seem to.

**Example (creative / collaboration).** You want to change a shared project's direction.
- You: want bolder, more original work.
- Collaborator: wants to ship on time and protect what already works.
- Client: wants it to look like the thing they approved.
- Audience: wants it to be clear and useful.
The conflict isn't "bold vs. boring" — it's "novelty vs. delivery risk." Now you can
design a proposal that speaks to that.

---

### Stringing them together
A solid "look before you judge" pass: **CAF** (map the field) → **PMI** (test the leading
idea) → **OPV** (check the people) → **C&S** (run the clock forward). Then hand off to
`debono-decide` to choose, or `debono-create` if nothing on the table is good enough. On
anything with real downside, add a **failure-inversion** pass (`debono-invert`) before you
decide — a sharper risk lens than PMI's Minus.
