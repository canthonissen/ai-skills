# De Bono — Invert (think backward from failure)

This skill adds **inversion** to the toolkit. Carl Jacobi, the 19th-century
mathematician, told his students *"man muss immer umkehren"* — "invert, always invert":
many hard problems are solved only when turned around and attacked from the far end.
Charlie Munger made it a life-and-business discipline: don't only ask "how do I succeed?",
ask **"how would I guarantee failure?"** and then refuse to do those things. As he put it,
*"All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there,"* and *"it is
remarkable how much long-term advantage we've gotten by trying to be consistently not
stupid, instead of trying to be very intelligent."*

**The switch.** Forward thinking asks *"how do I get what I want?"* Inversion asks *"what
would guarantee the opposite — and am I doing any of it?"* — then you avoid those things.
It doesn't replace forward thinking; it backs it up. Avoiding ruin is usually easier, and
higher-leverage, than engineering brilliance — especially when a failure is irreversible.

**Don't confuse it with Reversal (`debono-create`).** Both flip direction, but the purpose
and discipline are opposite:
- **Reversal** flips to *generate* ideas — judgment suspended, the flip is a stepping-stone
  you don't believe, the goal is creativity.
- **Inversion** flips to *solve and protect* — judgment fully on, the failure modes are
  real, and the output is a list of things to avoid or fix.

Pick the move by the situation:

| Situation | Move |
|-----------|------|
| Defining the aim; want to bound it | **Anti-goal** |
| Testing an idea/decision with real downside | **Failure Inversion** |
| A forward problem is stuck | **Jacobi reframe** |
| About to commit to a plan | **Pre-mortem** |
| Improving something; what to remove | **Via Negativa** |

## Anti-goal — name where you must NOT end up

**What.** Alongside the aim ("where do we want to go?"), state the **anti-goal**: the
outcome you refuse to allow.
**Why.** The aim pulls you forward but rarely tells you what to protect. The anti-goal
fences off disaster, and it often constrains the choice more sharply than the aim does.
**How.** Write the aim, then complete: "whatever happens, we must not end up ___." Treat
the anti-goal as a veto on any option that risks it.
**Example (work).** Launch aim: hit the date. Anti-goal: *never break trust with our first
100 customers.* That veto kills the "ship it buggy and patch later" option that the aim
alone would have allowed.

## Failure Inversion — "how would I guarantee this fails?"

**What.** Instead of only listing what's good and bad, actively design the disaster: *if I
wanted this to fail, exactly what would I do?* Then invert each item into a safeguard.
**Why.** "What's bad about this?" (a PMI Minus) is passive and misses things. "How do I
*cause* failure?" is generative and drags the real risks into the open.
**How.** Brainstorm the recipe for failure freely; then turn each ingredient into a "so we
will…" safeguard. Run it right after a PMI (`debono-explore`) on anything consequential.
**Example (personal).** New fitness plan. Recipe for failure: vague goal, no fixed days,
start too hard, no tracking, all-or-nothing. Inverted: one measured goal, three booked
slots, an easy first week, a simple log, "never miss twice." The plan designs itself out
of its own failure modes.

## Jacobi reframe — solve the inverse problem

**What.** When the forward question is stuck or fuzzy, solve its inverse instead — it's
often more tractable and far more concrete.
**Why.** "How do we get more X?" is abstract; "what makes people *not* X?" points straight
at fixable causes.
**How.** Restate the goal as its opposite, solve that, then translate the findings back
into actions.
**Example (work / growth).** Stuck on "how do we get more signups?" Invert: "what makes a
visitor decide *not* to sign up?" → slow page, unclear value, a long form, no trust
signals. Each is a concrete fix. The inverse was the lever all along.

## Pre-mortem — assume it already failed

**What.** Before committing, jump forward in time and assume the plan **failed badly.**
Write the autopsy: why did it fail? Then pre-empt those causes now.
**Why.** People defend a plan they're attached to; imagining it already dead gives
permission to see the real risks. (It's a focused, forward-aimed inversion.)
**How.** "It's [date] and this was a disaster — what went wrong?" List causes; for the
likely or serious ones, add a safeguard, an early-warning signal, or a fallback. This feeds
straight into `debono-act`'s monitor and contingency steps.
**Example (team / project).** "A year out, the migration was a fiasco — why?" Underestimated
data cleanup; the one expert left; no rollback. Pre-empt: scope the cleanup now, document
to remove the single point of failure, build a rollback. Risks become plan items.

## Via Negativa — improve by removal

**What.** Ask what to **stop, remove, or avoid** — not only what to add.
**Why.** Subtraction is often the faster, more reliable improvement, and it's cheaper to
not-do harm than to engineer good.
**How.** List what's actively hurting the outcome; remove or stop those before adding
anything new.
**Example (creative / writing).** To improve a draft, don't pile on techniques — cut the
clichés, the hedges, the throat-clearing intro, the stray adverbs. What's left is stronger,
and faster to reach than "add more craft."

## How it plugs into the framework

Inversion is the **backward lens** that runs parallel to the whole TO·LO·PO·SO·GO spine.
Use it wherever a forward tool would benefit from a failure-side check:

- **TO** — pair the **Anti-goal** with AGO (`debono-decide`).
- **LO** — run a **Failure Inversion** right after PMI (`debono-explore`).
- **PO** — use the **Jacobi reframe** when `debono-create`'s forward tools stall (keeping it
  distinct from Reversal).
- **SO / GO** — run a **Pre-mortem** before deciding (`debono-decide`) and to seed the
  monitor and contingency of the plan (`debono-act`); apply **Via Negativa** to prune it.

## One caution

Inversion is a complement, not a replacement — Munger's own rule is that you must *also*
think backward, not *only*. Over-applied it tips into risk-aversion, negativity, and
paralysis, and you can't avoid your way to a positive vision: you still need aims and ideas.
Use it to **bound and protect** the forward tools, not to smother them.
