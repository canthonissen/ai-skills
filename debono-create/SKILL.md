# De Bono — Create (alternatives & lateral thinking)

**Why a separate kind of thinking is needed.** The mind is a pattern system: it routes
new input down established channels. That's efficient but it traps you in the first
reasonable idea. Patterns are also **asymmetric** — once you see the new idea, the path
back looks obvious (that's why insight and jokes feel obvious in hindsight). **Lateral
thinking** is the deliberate practice of cutting *across* patterns to reach ideas that
ordinary step-by-step logic won't find.

**The key mental switch: movement, not judgment.** In normal thinking you judge ("is this
right?"). In the creative stage you use **movement** instead: "Where does this take me?
What's interesting about it? What's the principle? What does it open up?" You suspend the
verdict on purpose so an idea can act as a stepping-stone to a better one. Save judgment
for later (`debono-explore` / `debono-decide`).

Pick a tool by how stuck you are:

| Situation | Tool |
|-----------|------|
| Have one answer; want more | **APC** |
| Want to question "the way it's done" | **Challenge** |
| Have something that works; want variations on *why* it works | **Concept Extraction** |
| Totally stuck; need a jolt from outside | **Random Input** |
| Want a radical break / discontinuous idea | **Provocation (PO)** |
| Want to flip the problem | **Reversal** |

You don't need all of them. One good provocation or random word can crack a problem open.

## APC — Alternatives, Possibilities, Choices

**What it is.** The deliberate search for alternatives, *even when you already have a
satisfactory answer.* The belief to operate on: there are always more alternatives than
the ones you've seen.

**Why it matters.** The first adequate idea blocks the better idea behind it. You stop
looking the moment something works. APC forces you to keep looking past "good enough."

**How to do it.**
1. State the current answer or the thing to be explained.
2. Set a **quota** ("three more ways," "five alternatives") — a quota beats "think of
   some," because it makes you push past the easy two.
3. Generate to the quota without judging.
4. Work at the level that's stuck: alternative *actions* (what could we do?), alternative
   *explanations* (why might this be happening?), or alternative *perceptions* (what else
   could this be?).

**Example (work).** Sales are down. Don't stop at "the market is soft." Quota of 4
explanations: market softened / our follow-up got slower / a competitor changed pricing /
our best rep left / the product page broke on mobile. Each points to a different action —
and you'd have missed four of five by stopping at the first.

## Challenge

**What it is.** Point at the current way of doing something and ask: *"Why does it have to
be this way?"* — **not** because it's wrong, but to see whether it's the only way. You're
challenging the lock-in, not criticizing.

**Why it matters.** Most practices continue from history and habit ("we've always…"),
not because they're optimal. Challenge frees you to look even when nothing is broken.

**How to do it.**
1. Name the current method or assumption.
2. Ask: "Is this here for a real reason, or just continuity?"
3. Ask: "What if we couldn't do it this way at all?"
4. Don't defend the status quo; just open the door and see what's behind it.

**Example (everyday).** "Meetings are 60 minutes." Challenge: why 60? Because the
calendar defaults to it. What if the default were 25? You'd cut the padding and book
more, shorter syncs.

## Concept Extraction

**What it is.** Take something that works, pull out the **concept** (the function it
delivers, the "what it's really doing"), then find *new ways to deliver that same
concept.*

**Why it matters.** Copying a solution gives you one solution. Extracting its concept
gives you a generator of many. It moves you up a level, then back down a different path.

**How to do it.**
1. Find a working example (yours or borrowed from another field).
2. Ask: "What's the underlying concept here? What job does it do?"
3. Hold the concept; brainstorm other mechanisms that do that job.

**Example (creative / product).** A museum uses a "trail of footprints on the floor" to
guide visitors. Concept: *guide people without signs or staff.* Other mechanisms for that
concept: lighting that brightens ahead of you, a scent gradient, sound that gets louder,
floor texture changes. The concept, not the footprints, is the gold.

## Random Input

**What it is.** Introduce a **random** word or object that has nothing to do with the
problem, and force associations between it and the problem to open lines you'd never
reach head-on.

**Why it matters.** Working straight at a problem keeps you in the same pattern. A random
entry point drops you into a different part of your mind's network, and the route back to
the problem is a fresh path.

**How to do it.**
1. Get a truly random noun (point at a page, pick from a list, look around the room).
2. Don't reject it — randomness is the engine.
3. List the word's associations, then bridge each to the problem. Capture any idea that
   sparks, however odd.

**Example (de Bono's own).** Problem: improve an office photocopier. Random word:
**nose.** Nose → smell → the copier could release a lavender scent when it's low on paper
(a calm, noticeable "refill me" signal). You would never reach "scent alert" by thinking
about copiers directly.

## Provocation (PO) + Movement

**What it is.** Make a statement you *know* is wrong, impossible, or absurd — a
**provocation**, flagged with "PO" so everyone knows it's a stepping-stone, not a
proposal. Then use **movement** to extract something useful from it.

**Why it matters.** A provocation throws you out of the stable pattern to an unstable spot
you'd never arrive at logically — and from that spot a real idea may be in reach.

**How to set up a provocation** (pick one):
- **Reversal:** state the opposite of normal. ("PO: the customer serves the waiter.")
- **Exaggeration:** push a number to an extreme. ("PO: one delivery van carries one
  parcel.")
- **Wishful thinking:** state a fantasy. ("PO: the report writes itself.")
- **Escape:** deny something taken for granted. ("PO: shops have no prices.")
- **Distortion:** scramble the normal order or relationship.

**Then move** — don't judge. From the provocation ask: What's the *principle*? What's the
*interesting part*? What would follow *moment-to-moment*? What's the *value* hiding here?

**Example (work / service).** "PO: the restaurant has no menu." Movement: the chef cooks
to your mood/ingredients that day → idea: a short "tell us what you feel like" ordering
flow, or a daily surprise option. The absurd statement delivered a real, testable idea.

## Reversal

**What it is.** Take the normal direction of the situation and turn it around — backwards,
inside-out, upside-down — then see what the reversed version suggests.

**Why it matters.** Reversal is the fastest way to dislodge a fixed view, and it often
exposes an assumption you didn't know you were making.

**How to do it.**
1. State what normally happens, as a direction ("we push offers *to* customers").
2. Reverse it ("customers pull offers *from* us / customers make offers to us").
3. Ask what that reversed world would look like and what's usable in it.

**Example (everyday / personal).** Normal: "I tidy the house, then relax." Reverse:
"relax first, then tidy." The reversal exposes the real issue (you never relax because
the list is never done) and suggests boxing tidying into a fixed 15 minutes so rest isn't
hostage to it.

**Reversal vs. inversion — keep them distinct.** Reversal here flips direction to
*generate* (judgment suspended; the flip is a stepping-stone you don't believe). When you
instead flip direction to *avoid failure*, or to crack a stuck problem backward with
judgment fully **on**, that's **inversion** — use `debono-invert`.

---

### After creating
Lateral thinking is for *generating*. Once you have fresh options, switch hats: run them
through `debono-explore` (PMI/C&S) to test them and `debono-decide` (FIP, values) to
choose. New ideas are fragile — evaluate them *after* you've generated a few, not as each
one appears.
